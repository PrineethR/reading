# Oblique-Style Method Cards for a Comb-Shaped Design Practice

## How to Use This Deck
- Draw one card at the start of a project, a stuck moment, or a weekly reflection.
- Commit to acting on the card for that session or for a defined short window
- Treat the card as a constraint, not a suggestion: follow it even if it feels slightly wrong or uncomfortable.
- Afterward, quickly note what changed in your thinking or output as a result.

***
**1. Name the obsession, not the job.**  
Write one sentence that starts with “I can’t stop thinking about…” and let that guide the project, not your role title.

**2. Make the meta question local.**  
Take your broad question about humans, play, or perception and apply it to one tiny scene

**3. Draw the comb.**  
Map your skills as a comb: list each deep spike and write one line on how it changes the others.

**4. Choose today’s spike.**  
For this piece of work, pick one spike on your comb to be primary and let the others support it instead of competing with it.

**5. State your design claim.**  
Write a single, arguable sentence that begins with “Design is most powerful when…” and test all your decisions against it for this project.

**6. Refuse retro comfort.**  
When you notice you are copying a known style, ask: “What would make this impossible to have existed 10 years ago?” and push toward that.

**7. Design for one nervous system.**  
Imagine a single, specific person’s body and mind; optimize the experience for them first before generalizing.

**8. One deep year.**  
Pick one theme (e.g., “playful ergonomics” or “text‑driven interfaces”) and make it the secret focus of a full year of small experiments.

**9. Document before you move on.**  
Before you allow yourself to start a new rabbit hole, produce one page that captures what you learned from the last one.

**10. Ship one synthesis, not ten studies.**  
Combine three of your past explorations into a single, finished artifact instead of starting a fresh exploration.

**11. Decide the constraint first.**  
Like Eno, set a non‑negotiable limitation (time, materials, colors, behaviors) before you design; design inside the box on purpose.

**12. Switch instruments.**  
Do one full concept using your weakest medium (words instead of sketches, quick physical mockups instead of Figma, or vice versa). Accept the clumsiness.

**13. Start from the manufacturing problem.**  
Take one idea and rewrite it as a constraint from engineering, cost, or production, then redesign to satisfy that constraint elegantly.

**14. Kill the decoration.**  
Remove every element that doesn’t clearly serve perception, understanding, or use. If it’s only there to look “designed,” delete it.

**15. Iterate until boring, then one more.**  
Do variations until you are tired of them; the next variation after boredom is the one you keep.

**16. Write it before you draw it.**  
Describe the experience you want in 150 words as if it already exists; only then are you allowed to sketch or model.

**17. Three-sentence test.**  
Explain your idea in three sentences to a non‑designer. If they can’t rephrase it back, the idea is not ready.

**18. Publish smaller than you think.**  
Share an unfinished fragment (a paragraph, a diagram, a question) instead of waiting for a “proper” case study.

**19. Turn doubts into questions.**  
Every time you think “I’m not good enough at X,” rewrite it as a question and answer it in a page of writing.

**20. Title first.**  
Give your project a sharp, specific working title. Use it to keep the work from drifting into vagueness.

**21. Narrate the decision, not the outcome.**  
For each major design choice, write one or two lines about *why* you chose it; that becomes the spine of your documentation.

**22. Turn ergonomics into a game.**  
Design one interaction where the “right” ergonomic behavior also feels playful or rewarding, not just correct.

**23. CMF as a sentence.**  
Describe your CMF choice in one sentence that links material, color, and finish to a specific emotion in a specific context.

**24. Change one sensory channel.**  
Keep the function constant but alter only one sensory dimension (texture, weight, temperature, sound) and observe what that does to perception.

**25. Design the before and after.**  
For any object or experience, sketch or write what happens 5 minutes before and 5 minutes after use; design for those edges as seriously as the core.

**26. Break one rule of the room.**  
In a space (like a bedroom), alter one assumed rule (where light comes from, where soft vs. hard surfaces go) and follow the consequences.

**27. Measure arcs, not months.**  
When judging yourself, look at 3‑year arcs of skills and perspective, not the last project or job.

**28. Build one reference project for yourself.**  
Create a project that answers *your* main question, not a client’s brief, and treat it as a long‑term reference piece.

**29. Use teaching as a mirror.**  
Design a small workshop, lecture, or internal session. Notice which parts you can explain clearly and which parts feel fuzzy.

**30. Keep one foot in the lab.**  
Always maintain one ongoing experiment that has no client and no clear commercial outcome.

**31. Stay with the discomfort.**  
When you feel “this isn’t enough,” ask what skill or lens is missing, then deliberately construct one project to test that gap.

**32. Design the feeling, not the feature.**  
When working with AI, start by writing how the interaction should *feel* to a human, then decide what the model should actually do.

**33. Ban one convenience.**  
For a prototype, forbid yourself from using one standard AI capability (autocomplete, image generation, search) and see what else emerges.

**34. Treat AI as an intern, not a god.**  
Have the system do low‑level repetition or exploration; you still make the final framing and selection.

**35. Make one local, fragile thing.**  
Build or script something that only runs locally on your own machine, for you; explore what intimacy or control that gives you.

**36. Explain the system like a toy.**  
Describe an AI‑driven system as if it were a physical toy with rules, rewards, and boundaries; refine it until it’s teachable to a child.

**37. Archive the week.**  
Once a week, capture three artifacts: one thing you made, one thing you learned, one question you still have.

**38. Ask: what would I keep?**  
If you had to delete 80 percent of your work so far, which pieces or ideas would you keep? Why those?

**39. Show it to one more person.**  
Before shelving a project, share it with one additional human and ask them to tell you what they think *you* care about in it.

**40. Rename your practice.**  
For one day, stop calling yourself by your job title. Give your practice a temporary name that reflects what you are actually doing.
